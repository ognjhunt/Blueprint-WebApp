# San Jose, CA Final Launch Playbook

- interaction_id: san-jose-deep-research-final
- status: completed

## Executive Summary

San Jose should launch as a narrow, proof-led city motion built around one wedge: Exact-Site Hosted Review for industrial warehouses and advanced manufacturing sites.

The research supports three truths:

1. The city has concentrated demand for physical AI, robotics simulation, and deployment validation.
2. The city has a plausible exact-site wedge around modern industrial and advanced manufacturing warehouses in the Edenvale / Hellyer / Prologis corridor.
3. The company can only speak truthfully if it keeps the motion bound to rights-cleared capture, hosted review, and one concrete workflow lane.

This playbook is not a generic city-growth memo. It is the operating reference for one narrow launch system that humans and agents can execute without widening the motion before proof exists.

## Truth Constraints

- Blueprint is capture-first and world-model-product-first.
- The active wedge is Exact-Site Hosted Review.
- Private controlled interiors require explicit authorization before capture dispatch.
- No public claim may overstate rights, access, proof readiness, or buyer traction.
- No public claim may promise stack compatibility unless the relevant buyer stack is verified before outreach.
- Defense, export-controlled, or air-gapped review requirements are explicit constraints, not footnotes.
- The motion should stay at zero budget unless a human explicitly approves a different posture.

## What Not To Say Publicly Yet

- Do not say San Jose is already live as a city program.
- Do not say Blueprint has a complete citywide target list.
- Do not say the city has a finished proof-pack library unless the artifact is rights-cleared and published.
- Do not say the standard hosted-review path fits defense or export-controlled buyers without buyer-specific confirmation.
- Do not say the city can support standard procurement, pricing, or discount commitments beyond the approved human lanes.

## City Proof-Motion Thesis

San Jose is the best proof-motion city for Blueprint because the city co-locates physical AI buyers with warehouse and advanced manufacturing sites that can support exact-site world-model review.

The local demand cluster includes Applied Intuition, Scale AI, Fetch Robotics / Zebra, AutoX, Figure, IntBot, and Brain Corp as a stack-relevant buyer reference. The site cluster includes Edenvale Industrial Park, Prologis STEM Park, and named warehouse examples such as 550 Piercy Road and 6480 Hellyer Avenue.

The core commercial friction is the gap between buyer intention and a rights-cleared, browser-visible proof artifact. Blueprint closes that gap with exact-site capture, hosted review, and artifact handoff.

## Why This City Now For Blueprint

- San Jose is dense with embodied AI and robotics companies that care about physical site truth.
- San Jose has modern industrial real estate that is structurally aligned with robot workflow evaluation.
- The city can support a narrow exact-site wedge without pretending Blueprint needs citywide liquidity first.
- The local supply lane can stay small and curated if the company uses buyer-requested site access, site-operator intros, and existing lawful access where available.

## Lawful Capture Supply Acquisition System

The supply system should stay small, rights-safe, and specific.

### Recommended Access Modes

1. buyer_requested_site
2. site_operator_intro
3. capturer_existing_lawful_access
4. public_non_controlled_site

### Capturer Profile

- Site-authorized surveying, AEC, industrial inspection, or commercial mapping operators.
- Comfortable with repeatable indoor walkthrough capture.
- Able to document access path and rights authority.
- Able to follow a strict provenance and privacy checklist.

### First Capture Mechanics

1. Prefer buyer-requested site access when a buyer asks for a specific site.
2. Use site-operator intros when a warehouse owner or operator can authorize the path.
3. Use existing lawful access only when the capturer already has a valid rights path.
4. Use public_non_controlled_site only for bounded public-area capture where the brief preserves signage, privacy, and provenance rules.

## Rights / Provenance / Privacy Clearance System

- Every proof asset must carry a rights summary.
- Every proof pack must state what is approved, what is blocked, and what requires automatic policy review.
- Private controlled interiors require explicit authorization before dispatch.
- If the rights artifact is ambiguous, the pipeline should stop.

## Proof-Asset System

The first proof-ready San Jose assets should be:

- A browser-renderable exact-site world model.
- Downloadable artifact outputs such as point clouds, textured meshes, or semantic outputs where available.
- A hosted-session review surface.
- A compatibility manifest that explains what the buyer can do with the package now.

### Proof Pack Requirements

- Exact site or clearly labeled adjacent site.
- Capture date or recency window.
- Provenance summary.
- Rights and privacy boundary summary.
- Hosted-review entry point.
- Artifact handoff checklist.
- Explicit statement of what exists now versus what requires more capture or approval.

## Buyer Proof-Path Routing System

The buyer routing system should use one of three paths:

| Path | Meaning | Default next step |
| --- | --- | --- |
| exact_site | The requested site is available or can be captured within the operating window | Hosted review |
| adjacent_site | The requested site is not available, but the layout is close enough to be useful | Clearly labeled follow-up |
| scoped_follow_up | The request requires more negotiation, rights review, or special handling | Human-gated follow-up |

The first response should classify the path inside one business day.

## Hosted-Review Conversion System

- Start with the target site type and the buyer's actual robot workflow.
- Show only exact-site or clearly labeled adjacent-site proof.
- Attach artifact handoff expectations.
- Escalate pricing, rights, privacy, and commercialization questions to humans.
- Treat follow-up as a technical recap, not a generic sales email.

### Default Follow-Up

The default follow-up after hosted review should say:

- what site was reviewed
- what artifacts the buyer can inspect now
- what remains blocked
- what question needs a human
- what concrete next step is available
